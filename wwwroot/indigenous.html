Indigenous Groups in Sarawak

Sarawak is home to over 40 indigenous groups, each with its own unique traditions, languages, and lifestyles. The major indigenous communities include the Iban, Bidayuh, Melanau and Orang Ulu, alongside smaller but equally significant groups.

The indigenous groups in Sarawak are collectively known as the Orang Asal (meaning "original people") and make up a significant portion of the state's population.



IBAN (SEA DAYAK)



Iban is the largest ethnic group in Sarawak, making up around 30% of the population. They are historically known as fearsome headhunters, but now famous for their longhouses, weaving (pua kumbu), and traditional tattoos.


BIDAYUH (LAND DAYAK)



Bidayuh is primarily located in Kuching and Serian divisions. They are known for their bamboo-based architecture, farming, and hospitality. Their traditional baruk (round houses) were used for meetings and ceremonies. The Bidayuh celebrate Gawai Sowa (a thanksgiving festival) and maintain strong ties to their land and traditions. They have multiple dialects, but Bidayuh languages are distinct from Iban and Malay.



MELANAU



Melanau traditionally lived along coastal areas (Mukah, Dalat, Bintulu). They are historically fishermen and sago farmers. Some of them are still practicing Kaul Festival, an ancient ritual to appease spirits. Unlike other indigenous groups, some Melanau were among the earliest to convert to Islam, while others remain Christian or animist.



ORANG ULU (UPRIVER PEOPLE)



Kayan

They are skilled in boat-making and beadwork. They once practiced headhunting but are now known for their fine wood carvings.


Kenyah

Kenyah is known for their intricate tattoos, elongated earlobes, and beadwork. They are famous for sape music, a traditional lute-like instrument.


Kelabit

Kelabit is one of the smallest indigenous groups, residing in the highlands of Bario. They are famous for their wet paddy farming and salt production.


Penan

Penan are traditionally nomadic hunter-gatherers, but many have settled in villages. They are expert in blowpipe hunting and jungle survival. They are also one of the last groups advocating for rainforest conservation.
