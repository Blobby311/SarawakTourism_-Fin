Sarawak's Famous Food and Drinks

Sarawak’s food and drinks showcase a unique fusion of indigenous, Chinese, Malay, and Melanau influences, creating flavors that are both bold and distinctive.

From savory traditional dishes to refreshing local beverages, Sarawakian cuisine highlights fresh ingredients, aromatic spices, and unique cooking techniques. Whether it’s a hearty meal or a sweet treat, every bite and sip offers a taste of Borneo’s rich culinary heritage.



LAKSA SARAWAK

Laksa Sarawak is a famous noodle dish from Sarawak, known for its rich and aromatic broth. Sarawak laksa has a unique base created from a mixture of sambal belacan (fermented prawn paste), lemongrass, galangal and other spices, combined with coconut milk and chicken or prawn broth.

It is typically served with:

Rice vermicelli (bee hoon)

Shredded chicken

Omelet strips

Prawns

Beansprouts

Coriander and lime for garnish


To improve the flavour, sambal belacan and lime are frequently served alongside. It tastes well-balanced, with rich umami flavours, a hint of tang, moderate spice, and a creamy texture from the coconut milk.

It's one of the must-try dishes when visiting Sarawak, particularly in Kuching!


KOLO MEE

Mee kolo or kolo mee is a Sarawakian meal made of dry noodles, a flavourful mixture of pork (or chicken or duck for a halal version), shallots, and fried onions. The dish is then covered with a clear sauce, or Red Kolo Mee – dyed red with char siu sauce.

In Sarawak, Kolo Mee is a popular breakfast or lunch meal that is especially well-known in Kuching for having some of the greatest quality. This tasty noodle dish is a favourite among both locals and tourists, and it's served in both traditional coffee shops and busy hawker stalls.



MANOK PANSOH

Manok Pansoh or Ayam Pansoh is a traditional Iban and Bidayuh dish from Sarawak, featuring chicken cooked in a bamboo stalk, filled with water (which will later be the soup), seasonings and covered with tapioca leaves from the cassava plant over an open fire. The dish is infused with the natural aroma of the bamboo and enhanced with local herbs and spices, creating a uniquely smoky and fragrant flavor.

The natural bamboo infusion and lemongrass give the meal a somewhat sweet, smokey, and herbal flavour. It is usually eaten with steamed rice, which makes it an enjoyable and comforting meal.

In Sarawak, Manok Pansoh is often prepared for festivals, celebrations, and special occasions, particularly for the Iban and other indigenous groups.



KEK LAPIS SARAWAK

Kek Lapis Sarawak or Sarawak Layer Cake is a traditional layered cake from Sarawak. It is known for its vibrant colors and intricate patterns, which are created by stacking thin layers of cake, often in geometric or artistic designs.

The cake is rich, moist, and typically flavored with ingredients like pandan, chocolate, vanilla, or spices. Kek Lapis Sarawak is commonly served during festive occasions like Hari Raya, Gawai, or special celebrations.

It can be found almost everywhere in Sarawak and a favourite gift bought by visitors as it is available all year around.



UMAI

Umai is a traditional Sarawakian dish, especially popular among the Melanau people.

It’s a type of raw fish salad, made with fresh raw fish (usually mackerel, prawns, or anchovies) that is thinly sliced and mixed with lime juice, onions, chilies, and salt. The acidity from the lime juice "cooks" the fish, giving it a tender texture and tangy flavor.



DABAI

Dabai, or the scientific name, Canarium odontophyllum is a unique fruit native to Sarawak. Often called Sarawak olive, dabai looks like a small black olive with a yellowish flesh and a large seed inside. It has a rich, creamy texture and a slightly nutty, buttery taste when prepared properly.

Dabai is a seasonal fruit, highly prized in Sarawak, and can be expensive when in high demand.



TUAK

Tuak is a traditional rice wine from Sarawak, Malaysia, made by fermenting glutinous rice with yeast (ragi) and sometimes mixed with sugar or other ingredients. It is popular among the Iban and other indigenous communities of Sarawak, often brewed for festivals like Gawai Dayak (Harvest Festival), celebrations, and gatherings.

It has a sweet, mildly alcoholic taste when freshly brewed. Over time, as it ferments longer, it can become stronger and more sour. Some versions are infused with fruits, honey, or herbs for extra flavor.



TEH C PENG SPECIAL

Teh C Peng Special is a popular drink in Malaysia and Singapore, especially in Sarawak.

It's a three-layered iced tea made with:

1. Gula Apong (Nipah Palm Sugar) - A rich, caramel-like syrup at the bottom.

2. Evaporated or Condensed Milk - The middle creamy layer.

3. Strong Black Tea - The top layer, usually made from tea dust or local tea leaves.


When served, the drink shows distinct layers, but you mix it before drinking for a sweet, milky, and slightly smoky flavor from the gula apong. It's a favorite in kopitiams (coffee shops) and great for cooling down in hot weather!
